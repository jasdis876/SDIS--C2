Definition Contact Investigation (CI) is a public health strategy designed to identify, notify, test, treat, and prevent further transmission among persons exposed to communicable diseases. The Jamaican CI Programme focuses primarily on: HIV Syphilis Congenital Syphilis HEI (HIV Exposed Infants) Ophthalmia Neonatorum Hepatitis B Other priority STIs The Field Guide describes contact investigation as a process involving contact tracing, partner notification, testing, provider referral, and linkage to care. Unit 4: Syndromic Management Definition Syndromic management treats patients based on groups of symptoms and signs rather than waiting for laboratory confirmation. Why Jamaica Uses It Syndromic management is useful because: It allows treatment at first visit. It reduces loss to follow-up. It treats likely mixed infections. It is practical for primary care. It supports rapid public health control. Unit 1: HIV Overview Definition HIV stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus. It attacks the immune system, especially CD4 cells, and weakens the body’s ability to fight infections. HIV vs AIDS Term Meaning HIV The virus that causes immune system damage AIDS Advanced stage of HIV disease with severe immune suppression or AIDS-defining illness A person can live with HIV for many years without AIDS, especially with effective ART. Transmission HIV can be transmitted through: Unprotected vaginal sex Unprotected anal sex Sharing needles or sharp instruments Blood exposure Mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, delivery, or breastfeeding Occupational exposure HIV is not transmitted by: Hugging Sharing utensils Mosquitoes Casual contact Toilet seats Sweat or tears Public Health Importance HIV is important to CI work because: Some persons are unaware of their status. Newly diagnosed persons need urgent linkage to care. Partners may also be infected and unaware. Viral suppression reduces transmission. Partner services help identify undiagnosed cases. Stigma and fear can delay testing and treatment. 6. Unit 2: Natural History of HIV Disease Acute HIV Infection Acute HIV infection may occur 2–4 weeks after exposure. Unit 7: Adherence and Viral Suppression Adherence Adherence means taking medication as prescribed. Why Adherence Matters Poor adherence can cause: Detectable viral load Treatment failure HIV drug resistance Illness Continued transmission Viral Load Viral load measures the amount of HIV in the blood. Viral Suppression A person is virally suppressed when the viral load is low enough that HIV replication is controlled. U=U U=U means Undetectable equals Untransmittable. This means a person living with HIV who maintains an undetectable viral load on ART does not sexually transmit HIV. CI Counselling Message “Taking ART every day protects your health and can prevent passing HIV to sexual partners when your viral load is undetectable.” 12.
